04Deployment models

Four ways to cut infrastructure costs.

We're not selling a better MP3 — that already exists. We offer architectural flexibility: four deployment modes that replace today's 5-6 streaming bitrate versions with 1-2 well-defined copies, optionally bypassing backend entirely.

A.

Server-side reconstruction

Store only the compressed version. Servers reconstruct at delivery time. The client receives ready-to-play audio, with no changes to its flow.

~50-70 % less storage
Server CPU on smaller files
B.

Device-side reconstruction

The user's phone decompresses locally with the C++ engine. The server becomes a byte-delivery service. Compute distributes across millions of devices.

~50-70 % less storage
Server CPU near zero
C.

Two strategic copies

One bit-perfect version for archival and audiophiles, one max version for mass streaming. Replace today's 5+ bitrates with 2 copies, served by context.

Less transcoding
Fewer decisions, lower costs
D. NEW IN 2.0

Peer-to-peer · no backend

The .tfm travels directly between users via existing transports (messaging, email, P2P). Decoder is the app. No CDN, no streaming infrastructure, no licensing handshake at runtime.

Server cost ≈ zero
Cloud egress eliminated

06Frequently asked

What you're going to ask.

Is the C++ engine shipped, or roadmap?

Shipped in version 2.0. The Dart reference implementation was migrated to a native C++ engine with FFI binding. The .tfm format is identical between versions — a file produced by 1.x decompresses in 2.0 and vice versa. Decompression latency dropped to 15-25 ms for typical files (was ~106 ms in Dart).

The C++ binary is hidden-symbol stripped and includes anti-debug heuristics (frida-server, frida-agent, frida-gadget, linjector detection). Combined with Dart obfuscation in the Flutter layer, the asymmetric cost ratio between construction and functional reverse engineering is substantially increased.

How does the sharing actually work end-to-end?

The .tfm is a self-contained binary with a magic header (TRANSFM\0). The app registers an Android share intent and a content-resolver handler. When the user taps "share" on a .tfm, the system share sheet appears with any transport-capable app (WhatsApp, Telegram, Email, etc).

On the receiving end, the app validates incoming files by reading the first 8 bytes — not by trusting the filename. This matters because WhatsApp renames .tfm files to DOC-YYYYMMDD-... when it doesn't recognize the extension. The codec doesn't care about the name; it cares about the bytes.

Round-trip: compress in device A → share via WhatsApp → receive in device B → MD5 of decompressed output equals MD5 of the original WAV. Verified empirically on commodity Android hardware.

Why deliver an APK and not source code?

Because source code isn't the product. The product is the compression system, its native engine, and its protections operating together. Sharing isolated source would eliminate the layers that justify the licensing model.

We don't protect the algorithm by hiding it: we protect it by making copying economically irrational. Delivering the APK is itself a demonstration of confidence in the product's protections.

How do I know the benchmark isn't hardcoded?

Because the benchmark runs live, on your file, on your device. The app doesn't carry fixed numbers: it executes the real codec against the WAV you choose and reports what it measures. The expected vs obtained MD5 hash is computed on screen.

You can delete the original WAV after compressing and play the resulting .tfm: if it works, the decompression is real. Or share the .tfm to another device with a fresh install — same audio, same hash.

Does it compete with FLAC?

It competes in lossless compression on certain audio profiles (structured, voice, podcast) where TRANSFORMER can match or exceed FLAC. On dense musical audio, FLAC remains competitive.

The central difference isn't just the ratio: TRANSFORMER offers four deployment models (server, client, dual, P2P), end-to-end sharing, integrated cryptographic verification, and proprietary anti-reverse-engineering protections that FLAC, being open, cannot offer to a commercial platform.

What if someone decompiles it?

The binary incorporates multiple obfuscation layers (Dart AOT obfuscation with split debug info + native C++ with hidden symbol visibility + link-time optimization + symbol stripping) and an active protection system called Adaptive Integrity Shield that detects analysis environments and silently alters codec behavior.

Combined, the asymmetric cost ratio between construction and functional reverse engineering is substantial. It's defense, not secrecy: retains value even when fully known.

What about data center energy consumption?

Audio transmission and storage at scale are non-trivial contributors to data center electricity use. Compression reduces bytes transmitted, bytes stored, and bytes replicated across CDN edges — proportionally reducing the underlying electricity and cooling load.

For a platform processing one billion audio messages a day at 10 MB average, a 50% reduction translates to ~5 PB/day less transit. The exact CO2 impact depends on the electricity mix where the data centers operate (carbon-heavy grids gain more in absolute terms). We provide measurable byte-level reduction; carbon accounting is customer-side.

How much does licensing cost?

The licensing model depends on the use case: volume, integration, regional exclusivity, required support. We don't publish prices — they are negotiated by contract per partner's terms.
